7 Tactics To Expand Your Network 10x Faster (Use This To Win More Referrals):

1. Use The “Give First” Formula

Stop asking "Can I pick your brain?"

Instead, start offering value up front.

Share an article they'd find interesting with a personalized note.

Engage with their content on LinkedIn.

When you lead with generosity, people remember you differently.

2. The Coffee Chat Flywheel

After every coffee chat, ask: "Who else should I talk to?"

When they mention names, ask if they’d be willing to make an intro.

Most people will happily introduce you to 1-2 relevant contacts.

This simple question turns one conversation into multiple.

Always follow up within 24 hours to keep momentum going.

3. Daily LinkedIn Comments

Spend 15 minutes daily commenting on posts from creators in your target industry.

Add real value: share a resource, ask a thoughtful question, or add perspective.

After 3-4 meaningful interactions, send a connection request.

Reference their posts and how they’ve helped for higher acceptance rates.

4. Host Mini-Meetups

You don't need a huge event to be a connector.

Identify 4-5 people in your network who could help one another.

Invite them for coffee or a Zoom chat, then use ChatGPT to create an agenda and a facilitation strategy.

Rinse and repeat every month.

Being the host positions you as a hub in your network.

5. Make It A Daily Habit

Networking is most effective when you’re consistent about it.

Every week day, set a goal to:

Reach out to one new contact

Check in with an existing contact

Do that every day regardless of your job search or employment status and watch the results compound!

6. Build A “Second Brain”

You can’t scale your networking by keeping everything in your head.

Set up a Google sheet where you track:

Names

Emails

Notes from last conversation

Date of last conversation

This lets you easily recall facts about connections before you meet and plan who you need to engage with.

7. Follow Up Like A Pro

80% of networking value comes from the follow-up, not the first meeting.

Set calendar reminders to check in every week or two.

Always aim to add value - share an article, congratulate them on a win, or offer a quick update. Ex:

"Saw your team’s new product launch. How’s the response been over the first week? Rooting for you guys!”

## Post 2 31st March 2026

7 Red Flags Interviewers Use To Eliminate Candidates (And How To Dodge Them):

1. The Generic Answer Trap

Countless candidates get eliminated for giving cookie-cutter responses.

They say things like that could apply to any company.

The fix?

Research the company's specific challenges and tie your answers directly to their needs.

If you can swap in a competitor's name and your answer still works, you're in trouble.

2. The Negativity Spiral

You’re leaving your job for a reason.

But keep the negative employer talk out of your interview.

Interviewers see this as a preview of how you'll talk about them someday.

Instead, frame challenges as learning opportunities.

And frame any short comings as ways you got clarity on what you're excited about moving forward.

3. The Summary Slinger

Most candidates simply summarize their past experience.

But anyone can take an action or be “responsible for” something.

Hiring teams want to see the results that you drove.

Make sure your answers include measurable outcomes, multiple if possible!

4. The Preparation Gap

Walking into an interview without knowing basic company facts is a recipe for rejection.

It’s not enough to just browse the company About page and read a few news articles.

Spend at least 4-5 hours reviewing earnings calls, executive interviews, product reviews, social media channels, etc.

Aim to identify the company’s biggest goal, challenge, and initiative for the next 6-12 months.

5. The Body Language Betrayal

Your words might say "I'm excited" but if you're slouching and avoiding eye contact, you're done.

Great candidates lose offers because they appear distracted, disinterested, or their nerves completely overtake everything else.

Record yourself delivering a few answers, then watch to see what you can improve.

Even better, send the recording to a few friends for feedback.

6. The Salary Stumble

Dodging the compensation question or sharing a range that’s not aligned with the company’s budget means you’re likely not making it to the next round.

Before the interview, you need to do your research.

Check Glassdoor, H1Bdata, LinkedIn Salaries, Levels fyi, etc.

Build your range using market data.

Then share it with confidence on interview day.

7. The Follow-Up Failure

Thank-you notes are small things that make a big difference.

But most people don’t take two minutes to send one.

Don’t be that person.

Send a personalized note referencing specific conversation points.

Get it out a quickly as you can after the interview without sacrificing quality.
